I kept my eyes on my plate and chewed slowly to buy myself time.\n\n\"I asked one question,\" I said. \"She made it sound like I'd run off and joined the circus.\"\n\n\"Don't be flippant.\" His voice dropped, and that was worse than shouting. The quiet voice meant he'd decided something. \"A nursing program means Brennan. Brennan means a dormitory full of girls who don't know the Lord, and men who know them too well. It means you alone, two hours from this house, surrounded by every temptation I have spent your whole life keeping outside that door.\"\n\n\"It means I'd have a future,\" I said before I could stop myself.\n\nThe clock ticked. He laid his fork down again, slower this time, and the metal clicked against the china loud enough to make my shoulders draw up.\n\n\"Your future is here.\" He said each word like he was setting bricks. \"You play the organ. You teach the children their verses. When the Lord sends a man worthy of you, you'll keep his house the way your mother kept mine. That is the future I've prepared for you, and it is a good one, whether your books have convinced you otherwise or not.\"\n\nMy mother had kept his house until the cancer took her when I was eleven. She'd kept it quietly, in long dresses, with her own questions folded away somewhere I never got to see. I thought about that now, about the way she used to sit at the piano after he'd gone to bed and play something that wasn't a hymn, something with a strange minor turn to it, and how she'd stop the second she heard the floor creak.\n\n\"I'm not Mom,\" I said.\n\nHis face closed. He reached across the table, and for one second my whole body went rigid, but he only moved my anatomy text, which I'd left on the corner of the table that morning. He'd known it was there the entire meal. He'd been waiting. He picked it up, looked at the cover with its diagram of the muscular system, and set it down on the floor beside his chair, out of my reach.\n\n\"We'll burn this after supper,\" he said. \"And you'll thank me for it.\"\n\nThe roast turned to clay in my mouth. I swallowed it and reached for my water glass to keep my hands from shaking, because shaking hands gave him something to read, and I'd learned long ago to give him nothing.\n\n\"That book cost me eleven dollars,\" I said.\n\n\"Then it's an eleven dollar lesson in obedience.\" He smiled, and the smile was the part I hated most, because he meant it kindly. He believed every word. He thought he was saving me from a fire only he could see. \"Eat your supper, Jenna. We have evening service in two hours, and you'll lead the prayer.\"\n\nI looked down at the book on the floor, at the edge of its spine sticking out past his polished shoe, and I felt something hot climb up the back of my throat. I picked up my fork. I cut a piece of roast I had no intention of eating, and I kept my eyes on my plate while he said grace a second time, thanking the Lord for the strength to guide a wayward child.\n\nOutside, the wind had started, the dry Texas wind that scraped the fences and rattled the storm cellar door we never used. I listened to it instead of him. I thought about the highway out past town, the one that ran straight into the dark toward Brennan, and how my truck sat in the drive with a full tank of gas.\n\nI didn't know yet that the truck would break down on that road within the week. I only knew that I couldn't sit at this table much longer without doing something I couldn't take back.\n\n\"Jenna,\" my father said. \"You're not eating.\"\n\nI lifted my fork and made myself smile back at him, the same kind smile he'd given me, and I let him believe it."
